# Domain Pitfalls

**Domain:** Undervisning i kallkritik med fokus pa konspirationsteorier och AI-literacy (gymnasiet)
**Researched:** 2026-03-10

## Critical Pitfalls

Mistakes that cause the teaching module to backfire or fail its core objective.

### Pitfall 1: The Ridicule Trap - Treating Conspiracy Theories as Inherently Stupid

**What goes wrong:** The teacher or lesson materials frame conspiracy theories as obviously absurd, using a mocking tone ("how can anyone believe this?"). Students who hold or are sympathetic to conspiracy beliefs shut down, become defensive, and entrench further. Students who do not hold these beliefs learn contempt rather than analytical skill.

**Why it happens:** It is the path of least resistance. Most adults find flat-earth or QAnon claims absurd, and teachers unconsciously signal this through tone, facial expressions, and material selection. Selecting only the most extreme examples reinforces this dynamic.

**Consequences:**
- Students with conspiratorial sympathies feel attacked and become more committed to their beliefs (identity-protective cognition)
- The class learns to mock rather than analyze - a skill with zero transfer value
- The teacher loses credibility as a neutral facilitator
- The core pedagogical goal (intellectual honesty) is destroyed

**Prevention:**
- Use the phrase "granska, inte avfarda" (examine, not dismiss) as a mantra throughout the module
- Select conspiracy theories on a spectrum - include historical conspiracies that turned out to be true (MK-Ultra, Tuskegee, tobacco industry) alongside false ones
- Apply identical analytical frameworks to both true and false conspiracy claims - let the method reveal the difference
- Use steel-manning: require students to articulate the strongest version of a conspiracy theory before critiquing it
- Monitor your own tone - record yourself teaching if possible

**Detection:**
- Students giggle or use dismissive language about conspiracy believers during discussions
- Exit ticket responses show contempt rather than analysis ("people who believe this are dumb")
- Students with minority viewpoints stop participating

**Phase relevance:** Must be established in Lesson 1 and reinforced throughout. The introductory lesson sets the epistemic culture for the entire module.

### Pitfall 2: The Cynicism Overcorrection - Teaching Students to Distrust Everything

**What goes wrong:** Source criticism training focuses so heavily on "question everything" that students develop generalized cynicism rather than calibrated skepticism. They conclude that all sources are equally biased and untrustworthy - a state researchers call "trust compression" or "multiplist" thinking ("everyone has an agenda, so nothing is reliable").

**Why it happens:** Teachers emphasize deconstruction (finding flaws in sources) without equally emphasizing reconstruction (how to identify and weight trustworthy sources). The AI component amplifies this - if AI can generate convincing fake content, students reason, then nothing can be trusted.

**Consequences:**
- Students become epistemically paralyzed - unable to distinguish between NYT and a random blog
- Some students adopt a "post-truth" stance where evidence-based reasoning seems pointless
- The module produces exactly the kind of vulnerable thinker that conspiracy theories prey on - someone who distrusts all institutions equally
- This directly undermines democratic citizenship (a core aim of Samhallskunskap)

**Prevention:**
- Explicitly teach where to place trust, not just where to withhold it. Media researcher Mike Caulfield's framing: "Media literacy is about where to spend your trust - but you have to spend it somewhere"
- Include exercises where students successfully verify claims using reliable sources - not just exercises where they find problems
- Create a "trust spectrum" activity where students rank sources and justify placement - the point is differentiation, not blanket suspicion
- After each deconstruction exercise, follow up with: "So where CAN we find reliable information about this?"
- Measure this risk via exit tickets: watch for responses like "you can't trust anything" as a warning sign

**Detection:**
- Exit ticket responses show blanket distrust rather than nuanced evaluation
- Students refuse to accept any source as credible during discussions
- Students equate mainstream journalism with propaganda without being able to articulate specific differences

**Phase relevance:** Highest risk in Lessons 3-5, where AI content and conspiracy theories are combined. The teacher must actively counterbalance deconstruction with reconstruction from Lesson 3 onward.

### Pitfall 3: The Exposure Effect - Accidentally Spreading Conspiracy Theories

**What goes wrong:** By presenting conspiracy theories in detail in an educational setting, the teacher inadvertently exposes students to persuasive conspiratorial arguments they had never encountered before. The classroom legitimizes these ideas by giving them airtime. Some students leave the lesson more interested in conspiracy theories, not less.

**Why it happens:** Good teaching about conspiracy theories requires presenting their actual arguments - you cannot teach students to analyze something without showing it to them. But mere exposure increases familiarity, and familiarity increases perceived plausibility (the "illusory truth effect").

**Consequences:**
- Students who were not previously aware of a conspiracy theory become intrigued
- The school setting adds perceived legitimacy ("the teacher showed us this, so it must be worth considering")
- Parents may react negatively to their children coming home with detailed knowledge of conspiracy theories
- In worst case, the module seeds the very thinking it aims to prevent

**Prevention:**
- Use prebunking (inoculation) rather than pure debunking: warn students about manipulative techniques BEFORE presenting the conspiracy content. UNESCO research shows this order matters significantly
- Focus on structural patterns and rhetorical techniques rather than the specific content of individual conspiracy theories. Teach students to recognize "the conspiracy theory playbook" - unfalsifiability, appeal to hidden knowledge, scapegoating - rather than deep-diving into individual theories
- When presenting specific theories, always pair them with the analytical framework simultaneously - never let conspiratorial content stand alone even briefly
- Use historical/resolved examples where possible (tobacco industry cover-up, Watergate) rather than currently active conspiracy theories that students might search for afterward
- Consider the "familiarity backfire": explicitly telling students "this is false" can paradoxically make the false claim more memorable than the correction

**Detection:**
- Students search for conspiracy theory content on their phones during or after class
- Exit tickets show fascination rather than analysis ("I didn't know about [theory], that's interesting")
- Students bring up specific conspiracy theory claims in other classes or contexts

**Phase relevance:** Highest risk in Lesson 2 (when specific conspiracy theories are introduced as cases) and Lesson 3 (when AI-generated conspiracy content is examined). The inoculation framework must be established BEFORE these lessons.

### Pitfall 4: Identity-Threatening Confrontation with Believing Students

**What goes wrong:** A student in the class genuinely believes in a conspiracy theory (or their parent/family does). The lesson structure forces a public confrontation where the student must either abandon a belief tied to their identity and social group, or become the class's example of "someone who fell for it."

**Why it happens:** Teachers plan for an abstract audience ("students will analyze conspiracy theories") without accounting for the fact that 40% of UK teachers report encountering students who support conspiracy theories. The probability of having a believer in a Swedish gymnasium class is high, especially for theories around COVID, 5G, immigration, or historical revisionism.

**Consequences:**
- The student feels personally attacked and becomes defensive - reinforcing their belief
- Social dynamics shift: the student is either marginalized or rallies sympathizers
- If the belief comes from the student's family, the teacher is perceived as attacking the family - destroying the teacher-student relationship
- In extreme cases, safeguarding concerns arise or parents complain

**Prevention:**
- Never ask students to reveal their personal beliefs about specific conspiracy theories
- Frame all analysis as examining "claims" or "arguments" - never "what YOU believe"
- Use third-person framing: "Why might someone find this convincing?" rather than "Do you believe this?"
- Establish a classroom norm early: "In this module, we are all researchers examining how information works. No one is being tested on what they personally believe"
- Prepare a private response plan: if a student reveals they believe a conspiracy theory, address it one-on-one after class, never publicly
- Choose conspiracy theory examples that are unlikely to intersect with students' identity (avoid theories tied to ethnic, religious, or political identity groups in the specific class)

**Detection:**
- A student becomes visibly uncomfortable or defensive during case analysis
- A student aggressively defends a position during what was meant to be analytical discussion
- Other students start looking at or referencing a specific classmate during conspiracy theory discussion

**Phase relevance:** Critical from Lesson 2 onward. The classroom norms must be established in Lesson 1. The teacher should informally assess the class's existing beliefs before the module begins.

## Moderate Pitfalls

### Pitfall 5: The AI Demo Decay - Examples Become Outdated Within Months

**What goes wrong:** AI-generated content examples used in lessons become obviously outdated as AI capabilities rapidly improve. A "convincing deepfake" from 2024 looks amateur by 2026 standards. A "hard to detect" AI text from GPT-4 is trivially identified after students have used newer models.

**Prevention:**
- Build the module around principles and detection frameworks, not specific examples
- Plan to refresh all AI examples each semester before teaching the module
- Include meta-discussion: "The specific examples change, but what patterns stay the same?"
- Use current, verified examples at time of teaching - do not rely on pre-made materials more than 6 months old
- Consider having students generate their own AI examples in class as exercises

**Phase relevance:** Affects all lessons involving AI content (Lessons 1, 3, 4, 5). Materials should be reviewed and updated before each teaching cycle.

---

### Pitfall 6: False Equivalence Between AI Errors and Deliberate Disinformation

**What goes wrong:** The module treats AI hallucinations/errors and deliberate conspiracy theory creation as equivalent phenomena. Students conflate "AI sometimes gets things wrong" with "someone deliberately created this to mislead." This collapses the crucial distinction between incompetence and malice, between error and disinformation.

**Prevention:**
- Explicitly teach the taxonomy: misinformation (unintentional errors) vs. disinformation (deliberate deception) vs. malinformation (true but used to harm)
- When presenting AI-generated errors, always ask: "Is this a mistake or a manipulation? How can you tell the difference?"
- Create separate analytical pathways: one for evaluating AI output reliability, another for evaluating intentional manipulation
- Show that the same AI tools can be used both ways - an AI hallucination is different from someone deliberately prompting AI to create misleading content

**Phase relevance:** Most critical in Lesson 3 where AI and conspiracy theories are combined. The distinction should be introduced in Lesson 1 and reinforced throughout.

---

### Pitfall 7: Analytical Framework Overload

**What goes wrong:** The module introduces too many analytical models (source criticism criteria, argumentation analysis, logical fallacies, AI detection techniques, conspiracy theory patterns) without giving students enough practice time with each. Students can list the tools but cannot actually use any of them effectively.

**Prevention:**
- Limit to 2-3 core frameworks that students practice repeatedly across multiple lessons
- Introduce the primary framework (kallkritiska grundfragor) in Lesson 1 and apply it to every subsequent lesson
- Add complexity gradually - new tools should build on, not replace, existing ones
- Prioritize depth over breadth: it is better for students to deeply internalize one analytical framework than to superficially know five
- Use retrieval practice at the start of each lesson to reinforce the core frameworks

**Phase relevance:** Risk is highest in Lessons 2-3 (the "toolbox-building" phase). The temptation to introduce everything early must be resisted.

---

### Pitfall 8: The Seminar Assessment Rewards Performance, Not Understanding

**What goes wrong:** In the planned seminar examination (oral assessment), socially confident students who can argue fluently score higher than analytically strong but quiet students. The assessment measures rhetorical skill and personality type rather than source criticism competence.

**Prevention:**
- Provide written preparation scaffolds that count toward the grade (so quiet students can demonstrate understanding before the seminar)
- Design seminar structure with built-in equity: assigned speaking order, think-pair-share before whole-group, written reflections between rounds
- Create clear assessment criteria that distinguish between "talks a lot" and "demonstrates analytical thinking"
- Consider complementary assessment forms: written analysis + seminar, not seminar alone
- Allow students to submit written follow-up if they feel they did not demonstrate their understanding orally

**Phase relevance:** Directly relevant to the examination design. Must be addressed when planning the seminar structure (Lesson 4 is the first seminar, final examination may be separate).

## Minor Pitfalls

### Pitfall 9: Confirmation Bias in Example Selection

**What goes wrong:** The teacher unconsciously selects conspiracy theory examples aligned with their own political perspective - e.g., only right-wing conspiracy theories if the teacher leans left, or only government-related theories if anti-establishment. Students notice the bias and either dismiss the module as politically motivated or feel validated in their own biases.

**Prevention:**
- Deliberately include examples from across the political spectrum (anti-vax from both left and right, government conspiracy theories from both democratic and authoritarian contexts)
- Have a colleague review your example selection for balance before teaching
- Include at least one example that challenges YOUR OWN political assumptions

---

### Pitfall 10: Ignoring the Emotional Dimension

**What goes wrong:** Conspiracy theories are treated as purely intellectual/logical errors. The module ignores that conspiracy belief often serves emotional needs - belonging, meaning-making, coping with uncertainty, sense of agency. Students learn to identify logical fallacies but not to understand why conspiracy theories are psychologically compelling.

**Prevention:**
- Include psychological drivers early in the module: uncertainty, need for meaning, group belonging, sense of control
- Normalize these psychological needs: "These are universal human needs. The question is how we meet them"
- Connect to students' own experience: "When have you felt uncertain about something and wanted a clear explanation?"

---

### Pitfall 11: Swedish Context Blindness

**What goes wrong:** The module relies heavily on American or English-language examples (QAnon, flat earth, Alex Jones) that feel culturally distant to Swedish students. Students engage with the examples as curiosities rather than as something relevant to their own information landscape.

**Prevention:**
- Include Swedish or Nordic conspiracy theories and disinformation examples: anti-immigration narratives, COVID restrictions debate in Sweden, the "no-go zones" narrative in international media about Sweden
- Use Swedish-language AI-generated examples, not English ones
- Connect to platforms students actually use (TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat) rather than platforms dominant in research literature (Twitter/X, Facebook)
- Reference Swedish institutions students know (Internetstiftelsen, Medieakademin, Vetenskap och Folkbildning)

## Phase-Specific Warnings

| Phase/Lesson | Likely Pitfall | Mitigation |
|-------------|---------------|------------|
| Lesson 1 - Introduction | Setting wrong epistemic tone (ridicule or cynicism) | Establish "examine, not dismiss" norm explicitly. Use mixed examples including verified conspiracies |
| Lesson 2 - Conspiracy theory cases | Exposure effect + identity threat with believers | Use inoculation (prebunking) before presenting cases. Third-person framing only |
| Lesson 3 - AI + conspiracy combination | False equivalence + cynicism overcorrection | Teach misinformation/disinformation taxonomy. Always pair deconstruction with reconstruction |
| Lesson 4 - First seminar | Assessment equity + students defaulting to performance mode | Structured seminar with equity safeguards. Written preparation counted |
| Lesson 5 - Perspective analysis | Framework overload if new analytical tools added | Reuse existing frameworks, do not introduce new ones this late |
| Lesson 6 - Written analysis | Students reproducing surface-level analysis | Provide exemplars showing depth vs. surface. Peer review focused on analytical quality |
| Lesson 7 - Synthesis and reflection | Superficial "we learned a lot" without genuine metacognition | Specific reflection prompts: "What did you believe before this module that you now think differently about?" |
| All lessons | AI examples becoming outdated | Review and refresh all AI examples before each teaching cycle |
| All lessons | Political bias in example selection | Cross-spectrum examples. Colleague review of materials |
