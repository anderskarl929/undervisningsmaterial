## Recommended Pedagogical Stack

### Core Instructional Framework

| Framework | Source | Purpose | Why |
|-----------|--------|---------|-----|
| Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction | Rosenshine 2012; Hattie meta-analysis | Lesson structure and sequencing | Evidence base is exceptionally strong (scaffolding d=0.82, direct instruction d=0.59). The existing 6-fas lektionsmodell in /planera-moment already implements this. Retrieval practice, worked examples, and gradual release are built in. |
| Inoculation Theory (Prebunking) | van der Linden et al. 2020; Biddlestone et al. 2025; Cambridge Social Decision-Making Lab | Primary strategy for conspiracy theory resilience | Medium-to-large effect sizes for prebunking, compared to small effects for debunking. | SIFT Method (Lateral Reading) | Mike Caulfield / Stanford History Education Group | Practical source evaluation technique | Teaches what professional fact-checkers actually do: Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims. Simple enough for gymnasiet students, rigorous enough to be genuinely useful. Replaces outdated checklist approaches. |

|-----------|--------|---------|-----|
| UNESCO Teacher Guide on Conspiracy Theories | UNESCO 2022 | Classroom strategies and respectful engagement | The most comprehensive practitioner-oriented guide available. ## Critical Research Findings That Shape the Stack

### 1. Isolated Interventions Fail (HIGH confidence)

Uppsala University study (Nygren et al., published 2025, n=459 Swedish upper secondary students): Three different short interventions - including the Bad News game (inoculation-based) - showed no long-term effect on students' ability to distinguish credible from misleading news. This means a 7-lesson module MUST build cumulative skills with spaced practice, not front-load theory.

**Implication:** Every lesson must include retrieval practice from previous lessons. The SIFT method must be practiced repeatedly across different contexts, not taught once.

### 2. Prebunking > Debunking (HIGH confidence)

Meta-analytic evidence shows inoculation strategies produce medium-to-large effects while counterargument/debunking strategies produce only small effects (multiple meta-analyses, van der Linden lab). Prebunking works by building "cognitive antibodies" - students learn to recognize manipulation tactics before encountering them.

**Implication:** Teach the MECHANISMS of conspiracy theories (logical fallacies, emotional appeals, false pattern recognition) before analyzing specific cases. Lesson 1-2 should build this toolkit.

### 3. Teacher Strategies Often Backfire (HIGH confidence)

Jerome (2024, n=7691 UK teachers): 40% encounter conspiracy-believing students. Teachers use contradictory strategies. Ad hoc classroom arguments are particularly counterproductive due to backfire and bolstering effects.

**Implication:** The module needs structured protocols for handling student-expressed conspiracy beliefs - not scripts, but principles. The "intellektuell respekt" approach in PROJECT.md is research-aligned.

### 4. Analytic Thinking Helps But Is Not Magic (MEDIUM confidence)

Earlier research (Swami et al. 2014) showed analytic thinking reduces conspiracy belief. However, a high-powered 2024 replication found NO effect for simple priming tasks. The mechanism likely works through sustained education (van Prooijen 2017), not quick interventions.

**Implication:** Build analytic skills across the whole module (argument analysis, logical fallacy identification, evidence evaluation). Do not rely on a single "critical thinking" lesson.

### 5. Democratic Values Correlate With Source Criticism Skills (MEDIUM confidence)

Uppsala study found students who value democratic ideals perform better at fact-checking. This suggests connecting source criticism to democratic participation is not just curriculum-mandated but pedagogically productive.

**Implication:** Frame the module as democratic competence, not just media skills. Lesson 4-5 (samhallsdebatt context) already does this.

## Alternatives Considered and Rejected

| Category | Recommended | Alternative | Why Not |
|----------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Primary strategy | Inoculation/prebunking | Debunking/fact-checking first | Small effect sizes for debunking; risk of backfire with existing believers |
| Source evaluation method | SIFT (lateral reading) | CRAAP test (checklist) | CRAAP teaches surface evaluation (looks at the page itself). SIFT teaches what experts actually do (leave the page, verify externally). Research favors lateral reading. |
| Conspiracy theory approach | Empathic understanding of mechanisms | Ridicule or dismissal | Backfire effect is well-documented. Dismissal alienates students and can reinforce beliefs. |
| AI literacy scope | AI output as source to evaluate | Full AI literacy (how models work) | Out of scope per project brief. Technical understanding is neither necessary nor sufficient for source criticism of AI content. |
| Lesson structure | Rosenshine/6-fas model | Pure inquiry-based learning | For teaching specific analytical SKILLS (not exploring open questions), explicit instruction with gradual release is more effective. Inquiry elements appear in application phase (lektion 4-7). |
| Assessment | Summative writing + formative exit tickets | Traditional test | Source criticism cannot be meaningfully assessed via multiple-choice. Written analysis with source use demonstrates actual competence. Aligns with gy11 kunskapskrav. |

## Approaches to AVOID

### 1. "Spot the Fake" as Primary Pedagogy
Showing students fake content and asking them to identify it teaches recognition of specific examples, not transferable skills. Use as engagement hook (lektion 1) but not as the teaching method.

### 2. Presenting a "Correct" List of Conspiracy Theories to Reject
This positions the teacher as authority on truth rather than building students' own analytical capacity. Instead: teach the structural patterns (unfalsifiability, scapegoating, cherry-picking) that characterize conspiratorial thinking.

### 3. Assuming Students Are Blank Slates
Many students will have encountered conspiracy theories. Some may believe them. The module must account for both prebunking (for those who haven't) and respectful engagement (for those who have). The Jerome 2024 finding - that 40% of teachers encounter this - means it should be expected, not treated as exceptional.

### 4. One-Shot Digital Tools Training
Teaching reverse image search or fact-checking sites in a single lesson and expecting retention. Uppsala research directly contradicts this. These tools must be used repeatedly across lessons.

### 5. Treating AI-Generated Content as Categorically Different
AI content is ONE type of potentially misleading source. The same source criticism principles apply. Over-emphasizing AI's uniqueness risks creating a false distinction where students think "regular" misinformation is less dangerous.
